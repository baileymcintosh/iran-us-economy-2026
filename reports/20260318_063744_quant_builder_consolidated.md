### Chart 2 — TTF Gas: Dual Shock Anatomy
The TTF spike reflected a **compound shock**: Hormuz closure (cutting 20% of global LNG transit) **simultaneous** with Ras Laffan shutdown (removing another 20% of global LNG supply). Asian buyers lost their primary supply source, bidding up spot prices globally; at least 8 LNG tankers diverted from Europe to Asia, amplifying European gas prices. US Henry Hub rose only 7% — the US remained largely insulated due to lack of LNG export dependency.

### Chart 3 — Historical Comparison: Fastest Spike on Record
The 2026 Iran war produced the **fastest proportional oil price spike of any major conflict in modern history** — matching 2022 Russia-Ukraine's peak gain (+68%) in under half the time. The critical distinction: 2026 combined oil AND LNG disruption simultaneously. The 2003 Iraq War is a useful counter-example: oil *fell* as the conflict was expected to be short. 2026 shares more characteristics with 1990 Gulf War (chokepoint closure) but with greater speed and compound energy exposure.

### Agenda Item A: CPI Inflation Pass-Through ($71 → $119 Brent)

Using the Federal Reserve's standard oil pass-through framework (0.3–0.55 pp per 10% oil increase):

| Scenario | Pass-Through Rate | CPI Impact | Est. Headline CPI |
|----------|------------------|------------|-------------------|
| Low (brief spike) | 0.30pp / 10% | +2.0pp | **~4.8%** |
| **Mid (sustained)** | **0.42pp / 10%** | **+2.8pp** | **~5.6%** |
| High (prolonged Hormuz) | 0.55pp / 10% | +3.7pp | **~6.5%** |

Pre-war CPI baseline: ~2.8%. The gas pump confirms this: **$2.98 → $3.63/gal (+21.8%)** by mid-March — the fastest weekly gas price jump since Ukraine 2022. Diesel rose ~90 cents/gallon in one week. Deutsche Bank flagged a **"stagflationary shock"** risk. The mid-estimate of **~5.6% headline CPI** is the central projection, assuming the Hormuz disruption persists through Q2 before easing.

### Agenda Item B: SPR Activation & Remaining Capacity

- **Pre-war SPR inventory**: ~415 million barrels (end Q4 2025, per CRS/IEA)
- **Mar 11 release announced**: 172 million barrels (2nd-largest ever; Biden 2022 = 180Mb)
- **IEA coordinated total**: 400 million barrels from 32 nations
- **Post-release SPR**: ~243 million barrels — the **lowest level in 44 years** (since 1982)
- **Delivery timeline**: 120 days (~mid-July 2026)
- **Buffer capacity remaining**: At 243Mb, the US has roughly **24–30 days** of emergency cover at full Hormuz closure levels (~8–10 Mb/d shortfall), with *no meaningful reserve* thereafter
- The Trump administration claims ~200Mb replenishment "within a year" — but at current depressed SPR levels, any sustained second disruption would have essentially zero buffer

### Agenda Item C: SPR Release Impact on Oil Markets

The announcement produced a **-18.5% price reversal** from peak ($119→$97) within 4 days. However, this is below the 2022 Biden SPR effect (which helped drop Brent from $139→$96 over ~5 months). Key distinction: the **Hormuz near-closure is a supply flow problem, not an inventory problem** — the SPR release addresses domestic refinery supply but cannot reopen the strait. Macquarie warned prices could still hit **$150+** if Hormuz stays closed for several weeks. Goldman's post-announcement Q4 2026 forecast settled at $71/bbl — implying the market expects *eventual* conflict resolution, not permanent closure.

### Historical Comparison
The 2026 Iran conflict produced a **+67.5% price spike in 11 days** — exceeding Ukraine 2022 (+44.8% peak), though similar in trajectory to the Gulf War 1990 (+135% over 90 days). The key difference from 1990: the US is now a net oil exporter, providing some domestic buffer unavailable then.
