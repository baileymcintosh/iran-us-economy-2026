## Historical Precedents & Lessons

The research team identified four direct historical analogues, ranked by relevance:

1. **1973 Arab Oil Embargo (most structural parallel):** A politically motivated supply cut targeting consumer nations. Key difference: 1973's peak disruption was ~4 million bpd; today's is 8 million bpd — double the scale. Lesson: price controls and SPR hoarding made 1973 *worse*. Today's SPR release is the correct policy response, but the Atlantic Council warns against *repeated* SPR taps that erode the buffer's credibility.

2. **1990–1991 Gulf War (closest price behavior analog):** Kuwait invasion drove Brent from ~$17 to $40+/barrel within weeks before collapsing when the ground war proved swift. E*TRADE/Morgan Stanley analysis shows the current price action — back-to-back 3–7% daily moves — has occurred only **55 times since 1986**, with the last comparable streak in **2022**. In 1991, prices mean-reverted within 6 months. That precedent supports the "duration trade" thesis.

3. **2011 Libya Civil War:** Real oil price rose from $40.63 to $49.10/barrel; production fell 1.38%, implying an **inverse elasticity of 15.6** (World Bank/EIA, Verduzco-Bustos & Zanetti, 2025). IEA released 60 million barrels. Today's release is 6.7x larger — proportional to the far greater disruption.

4. **2022 Russia-Ukraine invasion:** IEA's previous record release of 182 million barrels. Brent peaked near $139/barrel in March 2022 before retracing. Russia found alternative export routes within months (China, India). Iran's Hormuz leverage has no equivalent rerouting workaround — making this crisis structurally harder to resolve quickly.

> **Critical lesson synthesised across all four precedents (MDPI/World Bank research):** Geopolitical oil price shocks have historically **caused more damage through uncertainty and precautionary stockpiling** than through the actual supply loss itself. The duration of uncertainty — not just the physical barrel deficit — is what drives recessions. Every week of Hormuz closure without a credible resolution timeline adds to systemic economic risk disproportionate to the physical barrel count.
