## Situation Overview

On **28 February 2026**, the United States and Israel launched coordinated airstrikes on Iran in what Thomson Reuters and the Congressional Research Service (CRS) have characterised as *the largest US military operation since the 2003 Iraq invasion*. Iran's immediate asymmetric response was not a counter-strike on US assets — it was the weaponisation of global energy infrastructure. Within 72 hours, Iran had deployed mines and short-range missiles in the Strait of Hormuz, the narrow passage through which **approximately 20 million barrels per day (mbd) of oil and LNG** flow — roughly one-fifth of total global supply (EIA, FactCheck.org, Goldman Sachs, March 3 2026).

The economic transmission was near-instantaneous. Brent crude surged from a pre-war close of **~$71.32/bbl** on 27 February to an intraday high of **$119.50/bbl** on 9 March — a **+67.6% move in seven trading days** (Reuters, Guardian). This is not merely a price shock; it is a simultaneous oil *and* LNG disruption. On **2 March**, Iranian drone strikes hit QatarEnergy's Ras Laffan Industrial City — the world's largest LNG export facility, accounting for **~20% of global LNG supply** — forcing a total production halt and declaration of force majeure (World Oil, TRT World, ModeEnergy). Dutch TTF gas prices surged **~70% in two trading sessions**, from €31.6 to a close of €53/MWh with an intraday peak above €60/MWh (ModeEnergy, 5 March 2026).

The conflict entered its third week as of this report's date (18 March 2026), with Iran's new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei publicly declaring that Iran would continue to "block" the Strait and attack neighbouring nations hosting US bases (Barclays/OE Digital, 13 March 2026). Iran has deployed approximately a dozen mines along the strait, materially complicating any reopening timeline (LSEG Senior Analyst Emril Jamil, cited in Barclays note). The EIA has revised its Q2 2026 Brent forecast to **~$91/bbl**, assuming *gradual* Hormuz transit normalisation beginning in April — an optimistic assumption.

## Key Findings

### 1. The Oil Shock: Speed and Severity Are Both Records

The quant research team's most important structural finding — **verified** across multiple independent data sessions — is that the 2026 Iran conflict has produced the fastest proportional oil price spike in the modern era. The comparable benchmarks, drawn from EIA and CRS historical data, place this in stark context:

| Event | Pre-Shock Price | Peak | % Rise | Days to Peak |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1973 Arab Embargo | ~$3/bbl | ~$12/bbl | +300% | ~180 days |
| 1990 Gulf War | ~$17/bbl | ~$41/bbl | +141% | ~90 days |
| 2003 Iraq War | ~$33/bbl | ~$40/bbl | +21% | ~30 days |
| 2022 Russia-Ukraine | ~$80/bbl | ~$128/bbl | +60% | ~21 days |
| **2026 Iran-US War** | **~$71.32/bbl** | **~$119.50/bbl** | **+67.6%** | **7–9 days** |

The 2026 event matches the 2022 Russia-Ukraine peak gain but achieved it in **less than half the time**. The Brent-WTI spread has widened to approximately **$8.30/bbl** (Mar 17), which the quant team correctly flagged as an anomaly. The explanation: US shale production — making the US a net oil exporter — provides some domestic WTI support from the supply side, while Brent bears the full Hormuz transit risk premium. This was identified as one of the core cross-agent findings: the US net-exporter status cushions *supply* but leaves *price transmission* fully intact. **Goldman Sachs confirmed (3 March 2026) that Brent closed at $77 on the first open trading day** of the conflict (from $72 pre-war), with their initial disruption risk premium model ($1–$15/bbl) already exceeded by the actual market outcome.

### 2. The LNG Compound Shock: Qual Verified, Quant Confirmed

This is the most critical cross-agent finding in the entire research session. The qual team flagged the Qatar Ras Laffan shutdown as a potential market-moving event. The quant team **confirmed** the TTF spike data with precision: TTF surged from **€31.6/MWh to €53/MWh close** (€62.5/MWh intraday peak on 4 March), a **~70% move in two trading sessions** — the largest two-day European gas price surge since the 2022 Russia-Ukraine energy crisis (ModeEnergy; Bruegel analyst Simone Tagliapietra: *"The threat to security of supply is here and now"*). US Henry Hub, by contrast, rose only **7%**, confirming the qual team's structural explanation: the US is not a significant LNG importer through Hormuz and its domestic market remains largely insulated from the transit closure.

The compound nature of the shock — Hormuz closure (disrupting ~20% of global oil flow) *simultaneously* with Ras Laffan shutdown (disrupting ~20% of global LNG supply) — creates a qualitatively different scenario from prior oil shocks. Goldman Sachs estimated that a **one-month shipping halt** through Hormuz could more than double European gas prices; their two-month scenario projects TTF above **€100/MWh** (World Oil, March 2026).

### 3. OPEC Bypass Capacity Is Structurally Insufficient

The quant team's bypass arithmetic, cross-verified with Goldman Sachs's IEA pipeline data (Goldman Sachs, 3 March 2026), reveals the fundamental supply gap:

| Bypass Route | Owner | Net Incremental Capacity |
|---|---|---|
| East-West (Petroline) | Saudi Aramco | ~4.5–5.0 mbd net (Yanbu port constraint) |
| ADCOP (Habshan-Fujairah) | ADNOC/UAE | ~440k bpd spare (Ruwais fire limits upside) |
| **Total bypass potential** | | **~5.5–6.8 mbd incremental** |
| **Hormuz normal flow** | | **~20 mbd** |
| **IEA reroutable estimate** | | **4.2 mbd** |
| **Structural shortfall** | | **12–16 mbd at full closure** |

Iraq (~4 mbd exports), Kuwait (~2 mbd), and Qatar (LNG) have **zero pipeline alternatives**. The quant team's bottom line: even maximising all bypass routes and OPEC spare capacity (~4.1 mbd nominal, of which OPEC+ activated a token **+206,000 bpd on 1 March**), the gap is structurally unclosable. The qual team explained Saudi Arabia's minimal output response as likely reflecting: (a) physical reservoir constraints on rapid ramping, and (b) rational revenue maximisation at elevated prices — Saudi exports via Red Sea routing are themselves price-beneficiaries of Hormuz disruption. Saudi Arabia was confirmed to have ramped output from **~9.0 mbd (October 2025) to ~9.8 mbd (late February 2026)** before partial disruption after Iran began targeting Gulf Arab infrastructure post-March 8 (BofA/Goldman data, confirmed by quant).

### 4. The SPR Response: Largest IEA Coordinated Release in History — But Structurally Insufficient

On **11 March 2026**, the IEA coordinated an emergency release of **400 million barrels** across 32 member nations — the largest in IEA history — with the US contributing **172 million barrels** of SPR (second-largest US release ever; Biden's 2022 release was 180Mb). The price effect was real: Brent retreated from its $119.48 peak to **~$97/bbl by 15 March** (−18.5% in four days). However, the quant team identified the critical constraint: the **SPR release addresses an inventory problem, not a flow problem**. It cannot reopen the Strait.

The post-release SPR level stands at approximately **243 million barrels** — the lowest since 1982. At a sustained Hormuz closure causing an 8–10 mbd shortfall, the remaining reserve provides roughly **24–30 days of emergency cover**, with no meaningful buffer thereafter. The Trump administration's claimed replenishment timeline of "~200Mb within a year" provides no near-term protection against a second disruption shock.

Brent re-firmed to **~$98/bbl** by the reporting date (18 March), with the EIA's revised Q2 forecast at **$91/bbl** on an assumption of gradual Strait normalisation. Macquarie warns of **$150+** if Hormuz remains closed for several more weeks.

As shown in the multi-asset stress chart from the quant research (Turn 2, March sessions), the S&P 500 declined from a pre-war level of approximately **6,879 to a trough of 6,632** (−3.6% peak-to-trough), then partially recovered to **6,716** (−2.4% vs pre-war) as of 18 March. Against a +67.6% oil shock, this appears contradictory. The quant data resolves the paradox through sector ETF decomposition:

Energy and Technology are co-leading the S&P 500. The index headline figure **masks extreme cross-sector dispersion**: airlines, consumer discretionary, and transport names are suffering sharply, while energy producers (earnings leverage ~2–3x oil price sensitivity) and AI/defense-tech names are rallying. The Brent-SPX correlation measured by the quant team since January 2025 is **−0.129** — nearly zero — confirming that oil's surge has had almost no *net* drag on equities. The Brookings Institution and IMF both warn, however, that this sector-rotation masking will not persist if the oil shock feeds into broader inflation and consumer demand destruction (Brookings and IMF, cited in qual research).

**The contrast with European and EM equities is geopolitically significant**: the Morningstar Europe Index fell −7% and MSCI EM −8.4% in the conflict's first five days (Star Tribune data, confirmed by RetirePath). Europe is disproportionately exposed through both oil imports and LNG dependency. The qual team's structural explanation: the US net-exporter status and lower LNG import dependency create a genuine, partial insulation — but not immunity, as FactCheck.org has verified.

### 6. Inflation: The Time-Bomb in the Data

The quant team made one of the session's most analytically important observations: **the February 2026 CPI print of 2.66% YoY is a pre-war measurement**. The war began 28 February — effectively the day after the measurement baseline. As MarketWatch noted, the March CPI print (due ~mid-April) will be the first to capture the full energy shock, and *"may be harder for markets to absorb."*

Using the Federal Reserve's standard oil-CPI pass-through framework (0.3–0.55pp per 10% oil rise), the quant team's scenario analysis projects:

| Scenario | Estimated Headline CPI |
|---|---|
| Brief spike / rapid resolution | ~4.8% |
| **Sustained disruption (base case)** | **~5.6%** |
| Prolonged Hormuz closure (bear case) | ~6.5% |

Gasoline has already moved from **$2.98 to $3.63/gallon (+21.8%)** by mid-March, with diesel up ~$0.90/gallon in a single week — the fastest weekly jump since Ukraine 2022. US consumers are absorbing this shock on top of a **26% cumulative price increase since 2020** (Star Tribune), making the political and economic tolerance for a sustained energy shock extremely limited.

### 7. The Fed Policy Bind: Stagflation Without Systemic Panic

The 10-year Treasury yield has *fallen* to **4.23% from ~4.5% pre-war**, while bonds (TLT) have sold off (−3.4%) and stocks sold off. This unusual "no safe haven" configuration was flagged by the quant team and is consistent with **stagflation pricing** — markets pricing both inflation risk (preventing safe-haven Treasury rallies) and growth deceleration risk (suppressing equities) simultaneously. The March FOMC rate-cut probability stands at just **4.7%** (CME FedWatch), with rate cuts pushed to September 2026 at the earliest. Deutsche Bank has publicly flagged a **"stagflationary shock"** risk.

Crucially, core CPI at **2.73%** is not yet showing second-round pass-through contamination. The Fed's window to assess whether the energy shock remains a one-time relative price adjustment or becomes embedded inflation is open — but closing rapidly.

### 8. Cross-Agent Verified Anomaly: Gold's Divergence

The quant team flagged a data anomaly across multiple sessions: **Gold's behaviour has been contradictory across different measurement windows**. In the longer Q4 2024 baseline comparison, gold appears up **+72.5–87.9%** to ~$5,000–$5,017/oz — a historic nominal record. In the shorter-window (from immediate pre-war), one quant session shows gold **−4.3%** from $5,230 to ~$5,006, while another shows it up +16.7% from ~$4,286. The resolution: gold had already been rallying strongly for months *before* the conflict (driven by central bank buying, dollar concerns, and AI-era fiscal expansion narratives), so the absolute level is a multi-factor phenomenon. The war added an acute safe-haven bid, but gold's near-term decline in some windows likely reflects portfolio deleveraging by institutional investors covering margin calls in other positions. The key signal is that **TLT (20-year Treasury ETF) is essentially flat (+0.6%)** while TIPS (TIP) are up **+6.8%** — the market is pricing inflation and currency debasement risk, not deflationary recession. This is precisely the gold-rising/bond-flat configuration of stagflation, confirmed by the Brookings Institution's long-term scenario analysis.

## Historical Precedents & Lessons

### 1990 Gulf War — The Closest Structural Analog
The 1990 Gulf War (Iraq invasion of Kuwait) is the most relevant historical comparison: a direct military conflict involving a major chokepoint, a large-scale US military operation, and an oil spike driven by supply-side disruption rather than demand surge. Oil rose **+141% over ~90 days**; a brief US recession followed. The key 2026 difference: the US was a *net oil importer* in 1990, making the domestic supply shock far more acute. Today, US net exporter status means the upstream sector benefits (XLE +34%) even as consumers pay the pump price (gasoline +22%). This creates the observed sector divergence in equities — a phenomenon not present in 1990.

### 2022 Russia-Ukraine — The Speed Benchmark
The 2022 conflict drove a comparable peak price gain (+60%) but over three weeks rather than seven days. The critical 2022 lesson: sustained elevated prices do not prevent eventual normalization if global supply chains adapt (US LNG to Europe replacing Russian pipeline gas). However, the 2022 precedent also shows that adaptation requires **6–12 months minimum** — a timeline during which cumulative inflation damage to households compounds.

### 2003 Iraq War — The Counter-Example
Oil barely moved (+21%, short duration) because the market judged the conflict as low-Strait-closure risk and short-duration. The fact that the 2026 conflict is *not* following this precedent — prices are sustained, not retracing — is itself the market's verdict on the structural severity. The quant team's analysis of the June 2025 Israeli strikes (+8.5% oil, quickly reversed) as a prior near-miss confirms that markets correctly distinguished between a precision-strike episode (June 2025: no Hormuz closure, no infrastructure strike) and a full conflict with chokepoint weaponisation (February 2026).

### The CPI Lag Lesson — Critical for Positioning
Every major oil shock has produced CPI readings that *understated* the impact in real-time. The quant team's identification of the February 2026 CPI (2.66%) as pre-war is the key analytical insight. Investors and policymakers who read that 2.66% print as evidence of contained inflation are operating on stale data. The true inflation impact begins landing in April (March CPI print), and if Hormuz remains effectively closed, will peak in May–June. The 4–6 week CPI lag is now the most important calendar event for Fed policy and equity positioning.
